Corrado Catania served as a director of Efforce Ltd (Malta) from incorporation in 2018 until at least 2021. Unlike the highly visible Andrea Castiglione or Jacopo Vanetti, Catania made no known public statements to the community and maintained a deliberately low profile.

As a registered director, however, he owed full fiduciary duties under the Maltese Companies Act (Cap. 386) — duties of care, skill, diligence, loyalty, and the obligation to prevent fraudulent trading. Catania is alleged to have remained passive while systematic misrepresentations were made to token holders, private-investor vesting was repeatedly breached, AitherCO2’s €319.9M tax liability was concealed, treasury tokens worth tens of millions disappeared without explanation, and the promised platform was never delivered.

His alleged prolonged silence and failure to intervene make him potentially jointly and severally liable for the resulting investor losses estimated at over $400 million.

Alleged Criminal Charges

1. Aiding and Abetting Fraud by False Representation

Malta Criminal Code Art. 308; Italian Penal Code Art. 640

As director, Catania allegedly knew or should have known of the systematic misrepresentations being made to token holders — AitherCO2 portrayed as financially robust, private investors as long-term institutions with “no intention to sell,” imminent platform launch — yet failed to correct them or resign.

2. Fraudulent Trading / Breach of Director Duties

Maltese Companies Act s.316A; Criminal Code Art. 307

Alleged failure to exercise oversight while the company engaged in conduct that was knowingly prejudicial to creditors and token holders.

3. Money Laundering / Concealment

Prevention of Money Laundering Act (Cap. 373)

Alleged failure to question or disclose the nominal €510 transfer of a €600M+ stake and layered treasury-token movements that may have been designed to shield assets from Italian tax authorities and creditors.

Alleged Civil Charges

  • Breach of Fiduciary Duty (Companies Act Art. 136A–142) — failure to act in good faith and in the best interests of the company while misleading marketing and undisclosed related-party transactions continued.
  • Breach of Duty of Care, Diligence and Skill — failure to monitor or challenge the disappearance of $68M+ in treasury tokens, the concealment of AitherCO2’s insolvency, and the non-delivery of the core product.
  • Negligent Misrepresentation / Material Non-Disclosure — jointly liable for omissions that induced investment (e.g., no disclosure of the €319.9M tax bomb, the true identity and intentions of the private investors, or the lack of any legal link between Efforce Malta and Efforce Bahamas).
  • Unjust Enrichment — potential personal benefit via direct or indirect shareholding while retail investors lost hundreds of millions.

What a Maltese Director is Legally Required to Do — And Allegedly Didn’t

Under the Maltese Companies Act (Cap. 386), every director must:

Art. 136A
Act honestly and in good faith in the best interests of the company.
Art. 136B
Exercise the care, diligence and skill that a reasonably prudent director would exercise.
Art. 142
Avoid conflicts of interest and disclose any interest in company contracts.
Art. 316A
Not allow the company to continue trading while knowingly insolvent or in a manner fraudulent to creditors.

Corrado Catania allegedly remained a director while:

AitherCO2, the company’s major shareholder, faced a €319.9M tax assessment and €408M loss — never disclosed to token holders.
AitherCO2 was repeatedly presented as the “mother company” and “parent company” of Efforce despite there being no legal parent-subsidiary relationship whatsoever.
Efforce Ltd (Malta) was publicly described as the “controlling entity” of Efforce Ltd (Bahamas) when no legal or shareholding link existed between the two entities.
AitherCO2’s 42.5% stake — worth well over €600M on any fair-market valuation — was transferred to an opaque Swiss entity (MBM Holdings AG) for a nominal €510.
The purported $36M raised in the private sale was never reflected as income or capital in the accounts of Efforce Ltd (Malta) or Efforce Ltd (Bahamas).
Over $68M in token movements from the Efforce treasury wallet (160.2M WOZX unaccounted for) were never recorded or explained in any set of company financial statements.
Efforce repeatedly and materially misrepresented private investors as “Asia-based cornerstone institutional investors” with no intention to sell, when in reality the entire 450M WOZX private allocation was controlled by a single U.S.-based investor who dumped hundreds of millions.
Treasury tokens worth tens of millions were moved without transparency or accounting.
The promised platform was never built, rendering the entire token utility false.
Private investors dumped hundreds of millions of dollars’ worth of WOZX directly contrary to repeated public assurances that they were long-term “cornerstone” institutions.
Despite clear evidence of the above wrongdoing, Catania allegedly failed to report any of it to the Malta Financial Services Authority, the Malta Business Registry, or any other competent authority as required of a company director faced with serious corporate misconduct.
Silence is not a defence for a director under Maltese law. Failure to act — and failure to report — is itself a breach.

Conclusion

Corrado Catania may not have been the loudest voice in the Efforce story, but under Maltese law that is irrelevant. A director who remains silent while €319.9M in tax liabilities are concealed, a €600M+ stake is transferred for €510, $68M+ in treasury tokens vanish, and $400M+ in retail losses accumulate is not an innocent bystander. He is a jointly and severally liable party who owed duties of care, diligence, skill, and loyalty — and allegedly discharged none of them. All allegations remain entirely unrebutted.
